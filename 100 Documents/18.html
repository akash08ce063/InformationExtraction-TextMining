Brain-Computer Interfaces

 

 

 


In the CNS lab at UNT, brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) allow for communication between a user's brain and a given simulation (e.g., virtual worlds ; virtual environments ; video games ). The BCIs are being applied in rehabilitation/training, neuropsychological assessment, and cognitive neuroscience projects. In the CNS lab BCIs are part of the adaptive virtual environments (AVEs) used for assessment and rehabilitation of cognitive and affective functioning (see below). Existing research in BCI applications includes two primary areas :

  1. Assistive technology : the BCI allows for persons with disabilities to regain functional capacities and improve their quality of life. 
  2. Rehabilitiation\training : the BCI may be used as a therapeutic application, in which patients recover their neurocognitive function through implicit and explicit alteration of their electroencephalographic (EEG) signals.

We are currently working with neuropsychologists (David B. Salisbury, Psy.D., ABPP-CN and Kimberley Monden, Ph.D.) at Baylor Institute of Rehabilitation to apply these technologies to persons with spinal cord injuries.

   Adaptive Virtual Environments:

   Assessment/Training

 




Adaptive virtual environments in the CNS lab make use of BCIs and psychophysiological interfaces to develop noninvasive human computer interactions with adaptive virtual environments (VE) for assessment and rehabilitation of cognitive and affective functioning. This project brings together a team of researchers to incorporate cutting edge neuropsychological and psychophysiological assessment into state of the art interactive/adaptive virtual environments. The CNS lab has developed a normative database and an adaptive virtual environment in which data gleaned from an assessment module is used for refined analysis, management, and rehabilitation of persons who have suffered brain injury (e.g., blast injuries) and/or are experiencing phobic responses (e.g., post-traumatic stress symptoms). This work was started while I was a faculty member at the University of Southern California and a Research Scientist at USC's Institute for Creative Technologies (see Parsons & Courtney, 2011 ). This work is continued in my CNS Lab at UNT (see Parsons & Reinebold, 2012 ). We have run over 400 subjects through these virtual environments. Subjects have been drawn from the University of North Texas, USC, West Point , Ft. Lewis, Madigan Army Medical Center , and Tripler Army Medical Center.

 

     Hardware/Software

 

 

The CNS Lab equipment now includes a number of different psychophysiological systems:

  1. Traditional EEG: 32 channel EEG system
  2. Biopac MP150 EEG: Allows for the recording of ECG, EDA, EMG, Respiration, and up to 16 leads of EEG. The Network Data Transfer (NDT) is a real-time data transfer system that allows access to the data being acquired for integration with third party applications
  3. Emotiv EEG: 14 channels (plus CMS/DRL references)
  4. EEGLAB toolboxes for advanced EEG signal processing: EEGLAB was developed in Dr. Scott Makeig's lab at UCSD. It is an interactive Matlab toolbox for processing continuous and event-related EEG.

     Invasive Brain-Computer Interfaces

     Impact of Deep Brain Stimulation:



My work with human-computer interfaces began with Medtronics's invasive brain-computer interfaces in the Neurology Department at UNC. I conducted research on the frontostriatal system and the cognitive and emotional sequelae of deep brain stimulation (DBS; see Parsons, Rogers, Braaten, Woods, and Troster, 2006 ; see also Woods, Parsons, et al., 2005 ). We did a meta-analysis to improve understanding of the variability and clinical significance of cognitive dysfunction after DBS. We found significant, albeit small, declines in executive functions and verbal learning and memory. Moderate declines were only reported in semantic and phonemic verbal fluency. Changes in verbal fluency were not related to patient age, disease duration, stimulation parameters, or change in dopaminomimetic dose after surgery. We found that STN DBS, in selected patients, seems relatively safe from a cognitive standpoint (see Parsons, Rogers, Braaten, Woods, and Troster, 2006 ). My current work focuses on the development of noninvasive brain-computer interfaces and psychophysiologically adaptive virtual environments.
